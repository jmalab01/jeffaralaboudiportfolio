The report describes the forensic examination of a damaged desktop computer recovered from a dumpster outside the former residence of Perry Winkler, a suspected drug trafficker actively investigated by Louisville Metro Police Department's drug enforcement team. The desktop tower showed obvious signs of deliberate physical damage; however, the internal hard drive remained intact and was properly imaged using forensically sound methods prior to being handed over for analysis.

This examination had as its primary goal the determination of whether digital evidence on the drive could indicate who used the machine, which drug-related activity might have occurred, whether efforts were made to conceal evidence, whether indications of planned flight existed, and what digital leads existed that would help investigators find the suspect or learn of accomplices.

The examination uncovered a wide range of evidence to link the device to Perry Winkler, including user account data, browser history, recent documents opened, and communication records. Several artifacts point directly to involvement in narcotics distribution, such as client lists, coded entries for drugs, and photos showing the packaging of narcotics. The system also contains signs of deliberate attempts to delete or wipe data, including evidence of a secure deletion utility and recently deleted folders related to financial transactions and inventory.

The artifacts about USB devices show that at least two external drives were attached to the system; both were probably carrying copies of client contacts, encrypted notes, and supplier communications. The most interesting discovery was a set of travel-related documents and searches that show the suspect planned to flee to Mexico, taking someone named "Tina" with him.

This report describes the methodology, findings, supporting evidence, and investigative leads recovered because of the examination. All findings are presented with the intent of supporting LMPD's ongoing investigation and any future legal proceedings.

 

The morning the search warrant was executed at the residence of Perry Winkler, the officers found an abandoned home devoid of meaningful physical evidence. Suspecting that Winkler had departed abruptly, investigators expanded their search to the surrounding area. A damaged desktop PC found in a nearby dumpster raised immediate suspicion due to its proximity and the suspect's known use of digital tools for communication and financial management.

Although the computer casing showed physical damage, the inner components, especially the hard drive itself, were still in a good state. The forensic team took the drive to the lab for proper imaging to preserve any recoverable information for analysis. Due to the nature of modern criminal activities, especially in narcotics trafficking, digital artifacts often provide more useful pieces of evidence by showing communication patterns, supplier contacts, records of transactions, location, and evidence of flight risk.

 

This examination was performed in a structured and methodical manner, following standards suitable for professional digital forensics. All actions were performed to maintain the integrity of the evidence and provide an in-depth analysis of the system files, registry hives, user activity logs, and deleted or hidden data that may exist.

It was vital to determine, prior to data examination, that the forensic image provided was complete, unaltered, and fit for analysis. It was necessary to verify the original E01 image file, using common hash algorithms. Resultant matching hashes indicated that the image had not been corrupted or altered after its acquisition.

Once integrity was established, the image E01 was mounted within a read-only environment to prevent accidental modification. The mounting of the image would facilitate navigation in the file system, extraction of relevant artifacts, and analysis at the system level by the examiner without endangering the integrity of the evidence.

Besides the verification of the image, metadata within the forensic container was reviewed. Timestamps, imaging tool logs, and acquisition notes indicated that the normal course of the imaging procedures had taken place. These steps are very important in establishing a clear chain of custody and ensuring that the digital evidence will be admissible in court.

This forensic analysis involved deep scrutiny of both user-generated and system-generated data. System-generated data includes registry hives, event logs, prefetch files, and OS configuration artifacts. User-generated data includes documents, web history, messaging logs, downloaded files, and multimedia content. Both kinds of data offer unique insights; together, they must be analyzed to form a coherent narrative.

The major components of the examination included:

1. System Information Review

Extracting OS version, installation dates, time zone settings, and user accounts.

2.         Registry Examination

Analyzing SAM, SYSTEM, SOFTWARE, and NTUSER.DAT hives to identify users, USB devices used, software utilization, and recent file activity.

3. File system analysis

Reviewing user directories, documents, downloads, pictures, as well as any hidden or deleted folders.

4.         Browser and Internet Activity

Analyzing web history, cookies, downloads, autofill entries, and search queries.

5. Application and Prefetch Analysis

Identifying run applications, including secure wipe ones.

6. USB Device Artifacts

Reviewing device connection history, serial numbers, and timestamps.

7. Messaging & Communication Logs

Data extraction from the default messaging applications, VoIP clients, and app data folders.

8.         Deleted File Recovery and Carving

Searching unallocated space for remains of deleted documents, logs, or images. The sections below outline the findings from each investigative question.

The second goal was to ascertain whether the user tried to conceal traces of prohibited activities or destroy digital evidence. The conducted examination revealed several system-level artifacts that indicated purposeful deletion by the suspect.

Secure Deletion Tool Identified

One such intriguing indicator was a prefetch file for a well-known secure deletion application called Eraser, used for wiping files beyond simple deletion. The file was found at the location:

•           C:\Windows\Prefetch\ERASER.EXE-*.pf

It clearly shows that the program was executed on this computer. Prefetch files are created by Windows to make applications launch faster on subsequent launches. However, they also store timestamps reflecting the last time a program was executed. This means that Eraser was actively run by the suspect shortly before abandoning the machine.

Registry Keys Confirm Installation

The SOFTWARE hive contained a subkey referencing Eraser:

•\software\\Eraser\\Eraser6

This, in effect, means the application was installed and not just run. The presence of application settings in the registry indicates that the suspect intended to use secure deletion more than once.

Recently Deleted Folders

Digital carving tools retrieved fragments of a directory entitled "Receipts_2025" that had several text files stored within it before it was deleted. These mention cash flow, customer payments, and quantities matching the amounts seen in the drug client spreadsheets elsewhere on this system. These had been deleted; however, their fragments remained within unallocated space.

Event Log Correlation

System event logs also revealed several application crashes, one of which included a failure event about Eraser. Incidentally, these events occurred within minutes of the deletion timestamps found during file carving. This correlation shows a strong likelihood that the suspect was trying to mass-delete files, and the system encountered errors during the process.

 

 

Together, these efforts at deletion indicate that the suspect attempted to cover his tracks and thwart potential forensic recovery.
